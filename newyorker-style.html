The Art of Silence: How We Lost the Ability to Think

In an age of constant noise, the luxury of contemplation has become a radical act.

By Jane Doe

The morning light falls in sharp diagonal lines across the desk of a writer in Brooklyn, but she doesn't notice. Her phone sits beside her, glowing with notifications—an email from her editor, a message from her sister, a news alert about something that happened in the world three minutes ago. She reads none of them. Instead, she stares at the blank page and waits for a thought to arrive.

This is becoming a rare moment. Not the waiting—we all wait now, suspended between distraction and intention. But the deliberate choice to sit with nothing, to let silence do its work. In the 1970s, a psychologist named David Lewis found that it takes the brain an average of twenty-three minutes and fifteen seconds to refocus on a task after an interruption. Now, with our devices designed to interrupt us every few minutes, we have become a civilization of people who have never finished a thought.

The silence we're discussing here is not the absence of sound, though that would be a start. It's a condition of mental sovereignty—the ability to close the door to the outside world, if only for an hour, and trust that your own thoughts are worth the investment. It's what philosophy teachers call contemplation, what writers call the work, what lawyers call billable hours, and what everyone else has learned to call the impossible dream.

Yet the question lingers: have we actually lost this ability, or have we simply lost interest in keeping it?

Jane Doe is a staff writer at The New Yorker, where she writes about culture and the mind.
